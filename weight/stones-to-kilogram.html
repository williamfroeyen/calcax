The kilogram, often abbreviated as kg, is a unit of mass measurement and is part of the metric system. It was originally defined as the mass of a specific volume of water at 4 degrees Celsius, but today the definition is more precise and based on fundamental physical constants.

Today, the kilogram is defined through Planck's constant (h), which is a fundamental physical constant in quantum mechanics. This definition was adopted in 2019 and ensures that the kilogram can be determined with extremely high precision, independent of physical objects. Before this definition, the kilogram was defined by a physical prototype stored in Paris, known as the "International Prototype of the Kilogram." This prototype was a platinum-iridium cylinder and was used as a reference to define the kilogram globally.

The kilogram is the basis for several other units in the metric system. For example, the gram (g) is one-thousandth of a kilogram, and the tonne is 1000 kilograms. In everyday life, we use kilograms to specify the weight of people, foods, goods, and other objects. For example, an adult person may weigh between 50 and 100 kilograms, and a common food package may weigh 1 kilogram. The kilogram is an important measure for science, technology, and trade, and a precise understanding of the unit is essential for accurate measurements in these fields.
